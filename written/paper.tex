\section{Introduction}
Despite the ongoing integration into established markets (whereas the last example would be the SEC's approval of a range of Bitcoin Spot ETFs), fundamental questions about crypto asset markets remain unclear:
How efficient are crypto asset markets relative to established, traditional markets such as equities? 
Does a risk-free rate exist and if yes, how does it relate to the risk-free rate in established markets? Are derivatives, written on crypto assets, fairly priced or at least fairly priced relative to other derivatives? 
We assess these fundamental questions and find that the methods required to conduct our analysis have been readily available; in fact we are employing similar methods to those, under which the efficiency of equity option markets had been assessed in their "youth" in the early 2000s. 
The common key behind the employed methods is the precise and bespoke tailoring of risk-return characteristics through the use of options, as proposed by Shumway and Bakshi et al in (@quote). 

We discuss the existence of a risk-free rate in crypto asset markets and derive a proxy. The proxy serves as a benchmark that beta-neutral portfolios should yield. 
Subsequently, we construct and evaluate the performance of beta-neutral option portfolios using a unique data set of executed transactions from Derbit, the largest Crypto Options Exchange.  We find that the beta-neutral option portfolios, written on Bitcoin and Ether, are fairly priced for most levels of moneyness. However, the portfolios do not generate the appropriate theoretical returns for at-the-money instruments. We compare the risk-return characteristics to the late 90s equity markets and find, in a similar fashion as the results reported by Bakshi et al and Shumway et al., the existence of high premia.for at-the-money options. The effect holds after compensating for tail risk (market crash insurance) and transaction cost.

\section{A Proxy for the risk-free Rate of Bitcoin and Ether}

One of the most fundamental concepts in mathematical finance is the existence of a risk-free rate and a risky asset. 
While crypto assets are well-known for their riskiness, the existence of a risk-free rate has not been established in this new market. 
The risk-free rate in established markets derives from the practice of lending out money to low-risk, typically government institutions which owe an interest rate on top of the principal to the lender. Despite the naming convention, government issued bonds are not entirely risk-free due to the possibility of default.
Although default risk is commonly mitigated by a government's possibility to create money, the chance of a sovereign debt default is non-zero and hence not entirely risk-free. 

In order to apply concepts of mathematical finance onto crypto assets, it is paramount to develop a risk-free rate - or at least a low-risk rate whose residual risk may converge to zero in time. 
An interesting candidate for a low-risk bond in the crypto asset space would be staking, which is the validation of transactions under the risk of losing a principal (the stake). Evidently, only crypto assets which follow a Proof-of-Stake (PoS) consensus mechanism instead of a Proof-of-Work (PoW) consensus mechanism can be used for staking. At the time, this is still a minor amount of cryptocurrencies. We include one of those, Ether, in our experiment. However, we figure that a multitude of risks is still present in staking, such as the risk of hacks, software bugs, slashing or blockchain congestions and subsequent reduction of stakes.
Another approach to develop a risk-free rate would be lending in the DeFi space. Yet, this space may be vulnerable to similar risks as staking through hacks or software bugs in smart contracts.

In the absence of riskless bond, we construct as a proxy for the risk free rate via the replication of the market-implied yield through a position in futures contracts, whose risks mainly arise due to liquidity risk and counterparty risk.
A large share of the trading volume in digital asset markets falls on the futures market. Among the futures trading, perpetual instruments have historically been and currently are the most liquid instruments. While it would be possible to use a yield from a long-short futures position (e.g. short a short-tenor future and long a longer-tenor future), we argue that in order to minimize liquidity risk, the most liquid instruments should be taken into consideration. 

Solve for interest rates from Futures price $F$ Spot price $S$ at time $t$ and time-to-maturity $\tau$:

\begin{equation}
F_t = e^{r \tau} S_t
\end{equation}


Crypto exchanges often provide a so-called perpetual future, which is a synthetic (typically cash-settled instrument) whose return is tied to the underlying via a funding rate. Most exchanges (quote here) provide perpetual futures for Bitcoin and Ether. 
The price of each perpetual future is linked to the underlying by adjusting a variable interest rate, called the funding rate, that fluctuates depending on the deviation between the price of the perpetual future and the underlying. The funding rate increases the cost of holding positions against the deviation, meaning that if the price of the perpetual future is higher than the underlying, the funding rate is positive which causes the market participants who are long to pay the short side in a predefined frequency. Broadly said, the more extreme the deviation, the higher the funding rate and thus the higher the incentive to trade against the deviation and thus converge the price of the perpetual future towards its underlying. 
The risk-free rate is approximated using the simplest and most liquid way of realising the funding rate, a carry trade. In such a trade, the trader shorts (longs) the perpetual future if the funding rate is positive (negative) and simulateously buys (sells) the underlying such that the market delta of the combined position is zero. 

The funding rate on \href{https://www.deribit.com/kb/deribit-linear-perpetual}{Deribit} is calculated as follows:

Instruments, such as inverse options, futures and perpetual futures, have a Mark Price $M$. The Mark Price at a time is defined as the average of the highest bid and lowest ask price within an unspecified bandwidth (quote: 
Mark price of an options contract is the current value of the option as calculated by the Deribit risk management system. Usually, this is the average of the best bid and best ask price. However, for risk management purposes, there is price bandwidth in place.
). The Deribit Index $D$, which is an equally weighted Index of BTC/USD pairs across different exchanges, is used in conjunction with the Mark Price to calculate the Premium Rate $P$ of the Perpetual Future $F$ over the Deribit Index. 

\begin{equation}
P = \frac{M - D}{D}
\end{equation}

The Funding Rate results as an 8-hour interest rate from the interval in which the Premium Rate falls. 

\begin{equation}
F = \max{0.025\%, P} + \min{-0.025\%, P}
\end{equation}

The actual funding payment results $f$ from the product of the Funding Rate, Position Size and Holding Time. It is continuously added to the PnL account and settled daily at 8:00 UTC.

\section{Methodology}

We follow the argument that any beta-neutral portfolio should yield the risk-free rate. We construct zero-beta portfolios by pairing a call option and a put option to a straddle and adjusting the weights so that the sum of option betas yields zero. The straddle's payoff $r_v$ is depicted in Equation 1, consisting of the payoff of the call $r_c$ and the payoff of the put $r_p$. The weight $\theta$ is found by solving Equation 2 through Equation 3. The (market) beta of a call (put) can be calculated through knowledge of the spot price $s$, the option delta $\delta$, the Black-Scholes option price $C$ and the market beta $\beta_m (=1)$ as depicted in Equation 4. 
The underlying data are executed transactions. All option greeks remain constant except time-to-maturity. Any transaction has a time-to-maturity (tau) "reset"/synced to settlement time. All options are bought and held until settlement at 10am UTC. 
We match our universe of straddles as follows:
Per definition, a straddle exists of a call and a put with similar strike and maturity. We construct the set of empirical straddles by iterating over our list of executed calls and puts. For each call (put), we use Put-Call-Parity to complete the straddle. 
The empirical performance of the resulting zero-beta straddles is assessed on a daily base. We inspect the densities (via Kernel Density Estimation) of the daily returns and argue that they are t-distributed. For each level of moneyness m, we construct an interval $[m, m + 0.05]$. We condition daily returns on over levels of the interval and we conduct a left-sided t-test on the null hypothesis of a population mean greater or equal to zero. We define a structural (or significant?) non-zero return of the zero-beta straddle returns as (volatility risk) premium. 

Straddle Return $r_v$占, Share of Straddles value in Call Options 占$\theta$, Call and Put market Beta 占$\beta_c$, $\beta_p$.

\begin{equation}
r_v = \theta r_c + (1-\theta)r_p
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\theta \beta_c + (1-\theta)\beta_p = 0\\
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\theta = \frac{-\beta_p}{\beta_c - \beta_p}\\
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\beta_c = \frac{s}{C} \Delta \beta_s\\
\end{equation}

We consider the possibility that if a volatility premium is found in the vanilla Zero-Beta-Straddles, then it may attributed to the risk of Black Swan events. Therefore we repeat the analysis for a set of "crash-resistant" zero-beta straddles.

\section{Results}

We find the returns of the zero-beta-straddles to be t-distributed and conduct a left-sided t-test under the null hypothesis of a population mean greater or equal to zero. 
The null hypothesis is not rejected for all models, vanilla and crash-resistant, for ITM and OTM options. Conversely, the null hypothesis is firmly rejected on a high significance level for ATM options. 
Therefore, we accept the alternative hypothesis of an underperformance for ATM options. Consequently, we find a similar result to Shumway and Bakshi - that is the existence of a premium for ATM contracts. 
We figure the premium to be a volatility premium since a straddle's payoff depends on the relationship of Implied Volatility to Realized Volatility. 
Unsurprisingly, the size of the premium in crypto markets is higher than the counterpart in equity markets - we figure that the reason is the higher risk in crypto markets, particularly via volatility and counterparty risk. 
Furthermore, there are mostly retailers present in the crypto option market. Some professional liquidity providers exist, but they remain few and therefore another reason for the premium's size could be illiquidity.
